Sorry to prolong this :)

Swadeshi has only one real meaning in Sanskrit. And, my point is, even if it was given a negative contextual connotation at some point in time -- especially a political one -- (which was not the case here as Ranjith explained), it shouldn't really matter. The tone is what is important. Your tone in the letter you wrote to the Editor was perfectly reasonable. And, so were the contents. That should be all that matters. If we start associating words/phrases with contexts and insist on using particular ones in preference to others, in my mind, we are expressing sentiments that aren't too different from those who insist on copyrights. Somehow, I've a feeling Gandhiji would have agreed.


Krishna.

Plus, it doesn't really make any sense to be picky about words. On the one
hand, we talk about free software etc., on
the other, we seem to imply that words or phrases are copyrighted.

What people claimed is that words are associated with meanings.  They
certainly do have meanings, but that is completely different from
being "copyrighted" (which they are not).

The issue that was raised is about *which* meanings these terms are
associated with.  It is a meaningful issue.  However, given Ranjith's
message explaining that Gandhi did indeed (as I was told before) use
the term "Swadesh", I'm convinced it is not a mistake for us to make
an analogy with it.  The point is to show people how "Don't buy
software from the colonial powers (businesses, today)" resembles
"Don't buy cloth from the colonial power (Britain)".

It could still be the case that some other terms would be clearer.
